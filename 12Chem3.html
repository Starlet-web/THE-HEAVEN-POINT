Electrochemistry

Electrochemistry is study of chemical processes that cause electrons to move. This movement of electrons is Called electricity, which Can be generated by movement of electrons from one element to another in a reaction known as an Oxidation-reduction (redox) reaction.

Application of Electrochemistry in daily life

  • Batteries (car batteries, digital watches, calculators)
  • Corrosion of industrial materials. e.g. Al, Cl2, NaOH...
  • Preparation of some chemical.
  • In analytical chemistry to determine concentration of ion. e.g. in blood.
  • In development of a certain disease.

Types of Cells

A] ELECTROCHEMICAL CELL :-

A Galvanic cell is a cell that can produce electricity with the help of chemical reaction.

B] ELECTROLYTIC CELL :-

An electrolytic cell is a cell that uses an electric current for the progression of a chemical reaction.

Difference of electrochemical cell and electrolytic cell

Electrochemical cell Electrolytic cell
Convert chemical energy into electrical energy. Convert electrical energy into chemical energy.
A spontaneous reaction occurs. A non-spontaneous reaction occurs.
The anode is negatively charged and the cathode is positively charged. The anode is positively charged, the cathode is negatively charged.

GALVANIC CELL :-

A galvanic cell is an electrochemical cell that converts chemical energy into electrical energy through a spontaneous redox reaction.. Some common example are Voltanic cell, Daniel cell, dry cell, leclanche cell.

Construction of Galvanic Cell

It consists of a copper vessel containing saturated copper sulphate solution (CuSO₄) and dilute sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄) as electrolyte. An amalgamated zinc rod dipped in zinc sulphate solution (ZnSO₄) acts as the anode, while copper acts as the cathode.

Copper sulphate crystals are kept in contact with the solution to maintain saturation. During the reaction, zinc is oxidized and copper ions are reduced, producing electric current.

Electrode Potential :-

When a metal rod is deep into its salt solution, a positive or negative electric potential is developed on the metal rod and an opposite electric potential is developed on the solution. Therefore, this potential difference between metal and its solution is known as electrode potential.

Step involve in daniel cell

In a daniel cell electrons flow from zinc electrode to copper electrode through an external circuit while metal ion from the one half cell to the other through the salt bridge.

The current flows from the copper electrode to zinc electrode thats is cathode to anode.

Cell Reaction :-

In Zn/ZnSO4 half cell Oxidation reaction occurs. (Anode)

$$Zn \rightarrow Zn^{+2} + 2e^{-}$$

In Cu/CuSO4 half cell, reduction reaction occurs. (Cathode)

$$Cu^{+2} + 2e^{-} \rightarrow Cu$$

The net cell reaction is

$$Zn + Cu^{+2} \rightarrow Zn^{+2} + Cu$$

Cell is represented by

$$Zn/Zn^{+2} \parallel Cu^{+2}/Cu$$

Salt Bridge :-

A salt bridge maintains electrical neutrality between the two half-cells by allowing the movement of ions. It is a glass tube filled with potassium chloride (KCl) or ammonium nitrate (NH₄NO₃) in gelatin form.

The gelatin permits ionic movement but prevents mixing of the solutions. The ions move equally due to similar ionic mobilities of cations and anions.

STANDARD ELECTRODE POTENTIAL :-

When a metal rod is deeped into 1 M solution of its ion at 298 k potential difference btween metal and solution is Called Standard electrode potential. It is represented by $E^{\circ}$.

There are two types:

1. Oxidation Electrode Potential :- (Anode)

Metal loses electron and becoming ions.

$$M_{(s)} \rightarrow M^{n+}_{(aq)} + ne^{-}$$

2. Reduction Electrode Potential :- (Cathode)

Ions gain electron to become metal.

$$M^{n+}_{(aq)} + ne^{-} \rightarrow M_{(s)}$$
$$E(cell) = E_{cathode} - E_{anode}$$ $$E^{\circ} = E_{Reduction} - E_{oxidation}$$

Q. Given the standard electrode potentials $E^{\circ}(Zn^{2+}/Zn) = -0.76~v$, $E^{\circ}(Cu^{2+}/Cu) = +0.34~v$. Calculate the standard EMF ($E^{\circ}cell$) of the cell $Zn/Zn^{2+} (1M) \parallel Cu^{2+} (1M)/Cu$

Sol.

$$E = E_{cathode} - E_{anode}$$

$$= +0.34 - (-0.76)$$

$$= 1.1~v$$

Measurement using the Standard Hydrogen Electrode

Measurement of standard Electrode Potential :-

Complete cell potential is use to measure the Electrode Potential of other electrode with a reference electrode. The standard hydrogen electrode is use as the reference (potential = 0v).

Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE) :-

The Standard Hydrogen electrode is a reference electrode with assigned potential of 0.00 Volt at constant and reproducible environment.

Construction :-

A Platinium electrode coated with platinium black is used. The electrode is placed in a solution of 1.0 M H ions (usually 1.0 M HCl). Hydrogen gas at 1 atm pressure is bubbled over the electrode.

Reaction at SHE :-

This setup insures a reversible redox reaction.

$$H_{2}(g) \rightleftharpoons 2H^{+} + 2e^{-}$$

This cell can acts as both anode (oxidation) and cathode (reduction) depending on the cell it is used in.

The Electrode Potential of SHE is 0.00 Volt at 298 k. This value used as a reference point to measure all the other electrode potential.

NERNST EQUATION & GIBBS FREE ENERGY

The Nernst Equation enables the determination of cell potential under non-standard condition. It relates the measured cell potential to the reaction quotient and allows accurate determination of equilibrium constants (including solubility constants).

The Nernst Equation is derived from the standard changes in the Gibbs free energy under standard conditions.

$$E^{\circ} = E_{reduction} - E_{oxidation} \quad ---(1)$$

$\Delta G$ is also related to E under general conditions (standard or not) via

$$\Delta G = -nFE \quad ---(2)$$

with n is the number of electrons transferred. F is the Faraday constant (96,500 C/mol). E is potential difference.

Under standard conditions, Equation 2 is then

$$\Delta G^{\circ} = -nFE^{\circ} \quad ---(3)$$

From thermodynamics the Gibbs energy change under non-standard conditions can be related to the Gibbs energy change under standard conditions via Equation

$$\Delta G = \Delta G^{\circ} + RT \ln Q$$
$$-nFE = -nFE^{\circ} + RT \ln Q$$

Divided by nF on both side

$$\frac{-nFE}{nF} = \frac{-nFE^{\circ}}{nF} + \frac{RT}{nF} \ln Q$$
$$-E = -E^{\circ} + \frac{RT}{nF} \ln Q$$

Multiply by (-) on both side

$$E = E^{\circ} - \frac{RT}{nF} \ln Q$$
$$E = E^{\circ} - \frac{2.303 RT}{nF} \log_{10} Q \quad ---(4)$$

$[\ln x = 2.303 \log_{10} x]$

At standard temperature $T = 298~K$, $R = 8.314$ and $F = 96,500$, the term $\frac{2.303 RT}{F}$ equals $0.0592~V$ and Equation can be written as

$$E = E^{\circ} - \frac{0.0592~V}{n} \log_{10} Q$$
$$E = E^{\circ} - \frac{0.0592}{n} \log_{10} \frac{[Product]}{[Reacted]}$$

Conductivity of Electrolytic Solution :-

Resistance:-

It is the obstruction to flow of electric current through the conductor.

OR

It is directly proportional to its length and inversely proportional to the area of cross section.

$$R \propto l$$ $$R \propto \frac{1}{A}$$ $$R = \rho \frac{l}{A}$$

where, $l$ = length, $A$ = Area of cross section, $\rho$ = specific resistivity.

Specific resistivity:-

Resistivity of a material is its resistance when it is 1 cm long and its area of cross section is 1 cm².

$$\rho = R \cdot \frac{A}{l}$$

Unit of Resistance $R$ = ohm ($\Omega$)

Unit of Resistivity $\rho$ = ohm meter ($\Omega~m$)

Conductance :-

The inverse of resistance.

$$G = \frac{1}{R}$$
$$G = \frac{1}{\rho} \times \frac{A}{l}$$
$$G = \frac{KA}{l}$$

where, $K$ = Kappa.

Unit of Conductance = $\Omega^{-1}$ (Sieohm, mho)

Conductivity :-

It is the inverse of resistivity.

$$K = G \frac{l}{A} \quad \left[ K = \frac{1}{\rho} \right]$$

Conductivity of a material is it conductance when it is one cm long and its Area of cross section is 1 cm².

$[A = 1cm^{2}, l = 1cm]$

$G = K$

Unit of conductivity = $\Omega^{-1} cm^{-1}$, $S~m^{-1}$

MOLAR CONDUCTIVITY

It is the conductivity produce by dissolving 1 gm mol of the all the ions of electrolyte in solution. It is denoted by $\Lambda_{m}$.

$$\Lambda_{m} = K \times V$$

Unit = $ohm^{-1} cm^{2} mol^{-1}$ or $S~cm^{2} mol^{-1}$

$$\Lambda_{m} = \frac{K \times 1000}{C (molarity)}$$

Conductivity decreases with dilution, while molar conductivity increases with dilution.

KOHLRAUSCH LAW

Limiting molar conductivity of an electrolyte is some of individual contribution of anion and cation of electrolyte.

$$\Lambda^{\circ}_{m}AB = \Lambda^{\circ}_{m}A^{+} + \Lambda^{\circ}_{m}B^{-}$$

Application of Kohlrausch

  • Calculation of limiting molar conductivity of weak electrolyte.
  • Calculation of degree of dissociation ($\alpha$)
$$\alpha = \frac{\Lambda_{m}^{c}}{\Lambda^{\circ}_{m}}$$
  • Calculation of dissociation constant of weak electrolyte
$$Kc = \frac{C\alpha^{2}}{1-\alpha}$$

Q. The conductivity of 0.20 M solution of KCl at 298 k is 0.0248 S cm⁻¹. Calculate its molar conductivity.

Sol. Given,

molarity = 0.20 M

$K = 0.0248~S~cm^{-1}$

$\Lambda_{m} = ?$

$$\Lambda_{m} = \frac{K \times 1000}{molarity}$$

$$\Lambda_{m} = \frac{0.0248 \times 1000}{0.20} = 124~S~cm^{2} mol^{-1}$$

Faraday Constant

Charge of 1 mole $e^{-} = 6.023 \times 10^{23} \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} C = 96500~C$

FARADAY'S LAW OF ELECTROLYSIS

1st law :-

The mass of any substance deposits or liberated at the electrode during electrolysis is directly proportional to amount of charged passed.

$$W \propto Q$$ $$W \propto I \cdot T$$ $$W = Z \cdot I \cdot T$$

where, $Z$ = electrochemical equivalent, $I$ = current, $T$ = Time

$$Z = \frac{E}{96500}$$

where, $E$ = equivalent weight

$$E = \frac{\text{Molar mass}}{n \text{ factor}}$$

2nd laws :-

When same amount of electricity pass to two different electrolyte connect in series then the mass of substance deposit at electrode is directly proportional to there equivalent weight.

Q. A solution of CuSO4 is electrolysed for 10 min with a current of 1.5 Ampere. What is the mass of copper deposit at cathode.

Sol. Given,

Time ($T$) = 10 min = $10 \times 60 = 600~sec$

Current ($I$) = 1.5 A

$W = ?$

electrochemical equivalent ($Z$) = $\frac{M}{n \times 96500}$

$CuSO_{4} = Cu^{+2} + SO_{4}^{-2}$

$M = 63.5, n = 2$

$$W = Z \cdot I \cdot T$$

$$W = \frac{63.5 \times 1.5 \times 600}{2 \times 96500} = \frac{57150}{193000} \approx 0.29~g$$

BATTERIES

Batteries are two types:-

  1. Primary Cell
  2. Secondary Cell

1.) Primary Cell :-

Cell in which reaction occur only once and then after use over a period of time it become died. Ex:- Dry cell, mercury cell.

2.) Secondary Cell :-

Cell which can be recharged by passing current through it in opposite direction, it can be use again. Ex:- lead storage battery, Ni-Cd cell.

3.) Fuel Cell :-

Those cells in which electric energy is produced by the combustion of fuels like hydrogen (H2), CH4, CH3OH, etc. Ex:- H2-O2 fuel cell.

Dry Cell :-

Also known as Leclanche Cell after its discoverer. Most familier cell mostly use in radio, remote, toy, etc. This cell gives a potential of 1.25 - 1.50 V.

This cell consist of zinc vessels at act as anode at which oxidation take place. Graphite rod is placed in middle of cell which is filled with a paste of NH4Cl + ZnCl2.

Graphite rod act as cathode at which reduction take place, which surrounded by powder MnO2.

Electrode Reaction :-

At Anode :

$$-Zn \rightarrow Zn^{+2} + 2e^{-}$$

At Cathode :

$$2MnO_{2} + 2NH_{4}^{+} + 2e^{-} \rightarrow Mn_{2}O_{3} + 2NH_{3} + H_{2}O$$

Net:

$$Zn + 2MnO_{2} + 2NH_{4}^{+} \rightarrow Zn^{+2} + Mn_{2}O_{3} + 2NH_{3} + H_{2}O$$

Mercury Cell :-

Smaller form of dry cell. This cell is mainly used in low current device like hearing aids, watches. This cell give a voltage 1.35 V.

This cell consist of a Zn container act as anode at which oxidation takes place. Graphite Rod placed in the middle of cell act as cathode at which reduction takes place.

vessel is filled with a paste of HgO + KOH.

  • Anode = $Zn(s) + 2OH^{-} \rightarrow ZnO(s) + H_{2}O + 2e^{-}$
  • Cathode = $HgO + H_{2}O + 2e^{-} \rightarrow Hg(l) + 2OH^{-}$
$$Zn(s) + HgO(s) \rightarrow ZnO(s) + Hg(l)$$

Lead Storage Battery :-

It is a Secondary cell used in automobiles and inverters.

This cell consist of six voltaic cell placed alternately. This cell give a voltage of 12 V in which three cell give 2V. So separated by thin fibre glass sheet.

  • Anode:- Pb plates and act as anode.
  • Cathode:- PbO2 and act as cathode.

38% dil. H2SO4 by mass is used as electrolyte.
