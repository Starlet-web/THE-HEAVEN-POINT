Electricity - Complete Class 10 Science Notes

1. Electric Charge

There are two types of electric charges:

  1. Positive Charge (protons)
  2. Negative Charge (electrons)

Fundamental Law of Charges: Like charges repel each other and unlike charges attract each other.

  • SI unit of charge is Coulomb (C)
  • 1 Coulomb = charge of approximately 6.24 × 1018 electrons
  • Charge on one electron = -1.6 × 10-19 C (negative charge)
  • Charge on one proton = +1.6 × 10-19 C (positive charge)
[Diagram: Interaction between positive and negative charges]

2. Electric Current

Electric Current (I): The rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor.

I = Q/t

Where:
I = Current (Amperes)
Q = Charge (Coulombs)
t = Time (seconds)

  • SI unit of current is Ampere (A)
  • 1 Ampere = 1 Coulomb per second
  • Current is measured using an ammeter (always connected in series)

Direction of Electric Current

  • Electrons flow from negative to positive terminal
  • Conventional current direction is opposite to electron flow (positive to negative)
[Diagram: Conventional current vs electron flow]

Example Calculation: If 2 × 1019 electrons pass through a conductor in 5 seconds, calculate the current.

Solution:
Q = ne = (2 × 1019) × (1.6 × 10-19) = 3.2 C
I = Q/t = 3.2/5 = 0.64 A

3. Electric Circuit

Electric Circuit: A closed path through which electric current can flow, consisting of a source of electrical energy (cell/battery), conductors (wires), and electrical components (resistors, bulbs, etc.).

  • For current to flow, the circuit must be closed (complete path)
  • If any part of the circuit is broken (open circuit), current stops flowing
  • Conventional current flows from positive terminal to negative terminal of the cell
[Diagram: Complete electric circuit with cell, switch, and bulb]

Circuit Components

Component Symbol Function
Cell/Battery [Symbol] Provides potential difference
Switch [Symbol] Controls circuit (ON/OFF)
Resistor [Symbol] Limits current flow
Ammeter A Measures current (series)
Voltmeter V Measures voltage (parallel)
Bulb [Symbol] Converts electrical to light energy

4. Electric Potential and Potential Difference

Electric Potential

Electric Potential at a point: The amount of work done in bringing a unit positive charge from infinity to that point.

  • SI unit: Volt (V)
  • Positive charge moves from higher to lower potential
  • Electrons move from lower to higher potential

Potential Difference

Potential Difference (Voltage): The amount of work done to move a unit positive charge from one point to another in an electric field.

V = W/Q

Where:
V = Potential difference (Volts)
W = Work done (Joules)
Q = Charge (Coulombs)

  • Potential difference is created by cells or batteries
  • Measured using a voltmeter (always connected in parallel)
  • 1 Volt = 1 Joule per Coulomb
[Diagram: Measuring potential difference with voltmeter]

Example Calculation: Calculate the work done to move a charge of 5 C between two points having potential difference of 10 V.

Solution:
W = V × Q = 10 × 5 = 50 J

5. Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law: At constant temperature, the current (I) flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference (V) across its ends.

V ∝ I
V = IR

Where R is the constant of proportionality called resistance

[Graph: V-I characteristics showing Ohm's law]

Conditions for Ohm's Law

  • Temperature must remain constant
  • Physical conditions (length, area, material) of conductor shouldn't change
  • Valid only for metallic conductors (not for semiconductors, electrolytes, etc.)

Example Calculation: A potential difference of 20 V is applied across a resistor of 5 Ω. Calculate the current flowing through it.

Solution:
I = V/R = 20/5 = 4 A

6. Resistance and Resistivity

Resistance (R): The property of a conductor to oppose the flow of electric current through it.

  • SI unit: Ohm (Ω)
  • Components designed to provide resistance are called resistors
  • Rheostat: Variable resistor used to change current without changing voltage

Factors Affecting Resistance

  1. Length of conductor (l)
    Resistance is directly proportional to length
    R ∝ l
    • Longer wire → Higher resistance
    • Shorter wire → Lower resistance
  2. Area of cross-section (A)
    Resistance is inversely proportional to cross-sectional area
    R ∝ 1/A
    • Thicker wire → Lower resistance
    • Thinner wire → Higher resistance
  3. Nature of material
    Different materials have different resistivities
    R ∝ ρ (resistivity)
  4. Temperature
    For most conductors, resistance increases with temperature

Resistivity (ρ)

Resistivity: The resistance offered by a conductor of unit length and unit cross-sectional area.

R = ρ × l/A

Where:
ρ = Resistivity (Ωm)
l = Length (m)
A = Cross-sectional area (m²)

Material Resistivity (Ωm) Nature
Silver 1.60 × 10-8 Best conductor
Copper 1.62 × 10-8 Good conductor
Tungsten 5.20 × 10-8 Filament material
Nichrome 100 × 10-8 Heating element
Rubber 1013-1016 Insulator

Example Calculation: A copper wire of length 2 m and diameter 1 mm has resistivity of 1.62 × 10-8 Ωm. Calculate its resistance.

Solution:
A = πr² = π(0.5 × 10-3)² = 7.85 × 10-7 m²
R = ρl/A = (1.62 × 10-8 × 2)/(7.85 × 10-7) ≈ 0.041 Ω

7. Combination of Resistors

Series Combination

  • Resistors connected end-to-end
  • Same current flows through all resistors
  • Total voltage = Sum of individual voltages
  • Total resistance increases
Rtotal = R1 + R2 + R3 + ...
[Diagram: Resistors in series]

Parallel Combination

  • Resistors connected between same two points
  • Same voltage across all resistors
  • Total current = Sum of individual currents
  • Total resistance decreases
1/Rtotal = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3 + ...
[Diagram: Resistors in parallel]
Property Series Parallel
Current Same through all Divides among branches
Voltage Divides among resistors Same across all
Total Resistance Increases (RS > R1, R2, ...) Decreases (RP < R1, R2, ...)
Applications Decorative lights, fuses Household circuits

Example Calculation: Three resistors of 2 Ω, 3 Ω and 5 Ω are connected (a) in series (b) in parallel. Calculate equivalent resistance in each case.

(a) Series: Req = 2 + 3 + 5 = 10 Ω
(b) Parallel: 1/Req = 1/2 + 1/3 + 1/5 = 15/30 + 10/30 + 6/30 = 31/30
Req = 30/31 ≈ 0.97 Ω

8. Heating Effect of Electric Current (Joule's Law)

Joule's Law: When current flows through a conductor, heat is produced due to the resistance of the conductor.

H = I²Rt

Where:
H = Heat energy (Joules)
I = Current (Amperes)
R = Resistance (Ohms)
t = Time (seconds)

Factors Affecting Heat Produced

  1. Current (I): H ∝ I² (Heat increases with square of current)
  2. Resistance (R): H ∝ R (Directly proportional)
  3. Time (t): H ∝ t (Directly proportional)
[Diagram: Heating effect demonstration]

Practical Applications

  • Electric heating appliances: Iron, toaster, heater, oven
  • Electric bulb: Tungsten filament heats up to produce light
  • Fuse: Safety device that melts when excess current flows
  • Electric kettle: Heating water

Example Calculation: Calculate the heat produced when 2 A current flows through a 10 Ω resistor for 5 minutes.

Solution:
t = 5 × 60 = 300 s
H = I²Rt = (2)² × 10 × 300 = 4 × 10 × 300 = 12000 J

9. Electric Power

Electric Power: The rate at which electrical energy is consumed or the rate at which work is done by electricity.

P = VI = I²R = V²/R

Where:
P = Power (Watts)
V = Voltage (Volts)
I = Current (Amperes)
R = Resistance (Ohms)

  • SI unit: Watt (W)
  • 1 Watt = 1 Joule per second
  • Commercial unit: kilowatt-hour (kWh)
    1 kWh = 1000 W × 3600 s = 3.6 × 106 J
  • Electricity bills are calculated based on kWh (units) consumed

Electric Energy

Energy = Power × Time
E = P × t
[Diagram: Electric meter showing kWh consumption]

Example Calculation: An electric bulb rated 100 W, 220 V is used for 5 hours daily. Calculate (a) its resistance (b) energy consumed in 30 days.

(a) R = V²/P = (220)²/100 = 48400/100 = 484 Ω
(b) Energy per day = P × t = 100 W × 5 h = 500 Wh = 0.5 kWh
Energy in 30 days = 0.5 × 30 = 15 kWh (units)

10. Practical Applications and Safety

Electric Bulb

  • Filament made of tungsten (high melting point: 3380°C)
  • Filament heats up to about 2500°C to produce light
  • Bulb filled with inert gas (nitrogen/argon) to prevent oxidation
  • Most of the energy is converted to heat (only 5-10% to light)
[Diagram: Incandescent bulb structure]

Fuse

  • Safety device to protect circuits from overloading
  • Contains a wire that melts when current exceeds rated value
  • Connected in series with the live wire
  • Rating examples: 5A, 15A, etc.

Household Wiring

  • Parallel connection used in homes
  • Advantages:
    • Each appliance gets full voltage
    • If one appliance fails, others keep working
    • Can control appliances independently
  • Three wires:
    • Live wire (red/brown) - carries current to appliance
    • Neutral wire (black/blue) - completes circuit
    • Earth wire (green) - safety wire to prevent shocks
[Diagram: 3-pin plug and household wiring]

Safety Measures

  • Use proper insulation on wires
  • Never touch electrical appliances with wet hands
  • Use proper rated fuses
  • Ensure proper earthing
  • Turn off power before electrical repairs
