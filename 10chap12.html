Conductors and Insulators

üîå Conductors

Materials that allow electric charge to flow through them easily. Electrons are free to move anywhere in the material. All metals are good conductors. Examples: copper, aluminum, silver, gold.

üö´ Insulators

Materials that do not allow electric charge to flow through them. Electrons are not free to move and are fixed in position. Examples: plastic, wood, rubber, glass, ceramic, dry air.

Key Difference: Conductors have free electrons that can move throughout the material, while insulators have electrons tightly bound to their atoms.

Electric Current (I)

Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge through a cross-sectional area. It represents how much charge passes through a point per unit time.

Electric Current Formula
I = Q / t

Where: I = current, Q = charge, t = time

SI unit of current: Ampere (A)

üìè 1 Ampere Definition

One ampere is defined as one coulomb of charge flowing per second through a cross-section of a conductor.

1 A = 1 C / 1 s

Potential Difference (V)

‚ö° Electric Potential Difference

The work done per unit charge in moving a small positive test charge from one point to another in an electric field. It's the "electrical pressure" that drives current through a circuit.

Potential Difference Formula
V = W / Q

Where: V = potential difference (Volts), W = work done (Joules), Q = charge (Coulombs)

SI unit: Volt (V)

‚ö° 1 Volt Definition

One volt is defined as when 1 joule of work is done to move 1 coulomb of charge between two points.

1 V = 1 J / 1 C

Cell and Battery

A cell produces potential difference by chemical action. Current flows from positive to negative terminal. A battery is a group of two or more cells.

Electric Circuit

üîå Circuit Definition

An electric circuit is a closed path along which electric current can flow, consisting of a power source, conductors, and electrical components. A complete circuit is required for continuous current flow.

Important: Current flows only in a closed circuit with potential difference. Open circuit ‚Üí infinite resistance; short circuit ‚Üí nearly zero resistance.

Series and Parallel Connections

üîó Series Connection

Resistors connected end-to-end so that the same current flows through each resistor. There is only one path for current to flow.

‚´ª Parallel Connection

Resistors connected between the same two points so that each has the same potential difference. There are multiple paths for current to flow.

Practical Tip:

Household appliances are connected in parallel so each works independently, gets the same voltage (220 V), and failure of one does not affect others.

Heating Effect of Electric Current

üî• Joule's Law of Heating

When electric current flows through a conductor, the electrical energy is converted into heat energy due to resistance. This phenomenon is known as the heating effect of electric current, discovered by James Prescott Joule.

Joule's Law Formula
H = I¬≤ √ó R √ó t

Where: H = heat, I = current, R = resistance, t = time

Derivation: W = VIt = (IR)It = I¬≤Rt
Since all work done is converted to heat: H = I¬≤Rt

Factors Affecting Heat Production

  • Directly proportional to square of current (I¬≤) - Doubling current quadruples heat
  • Directly proportional to resistance (R) - Higher resistance produces more heat
  • Directly proportional to time (t) - Longer time means more heat

Practical Insight: This heating effect is why wires get warm when carrying current, and why high-current devices need thicker wires to prevent overheating. It's also the principle behind many heating appliances.

Applications of Heating Effect

1. Electric Iron

Contains a heating coil (nichrome) placed in grooves. Mica insulation separates the coil from the metal plate. A bimetallic thermostat automatically breaks the circuit when desired temperature is reached (indicated by red light turning on/off).

2. Electric Bulb

Tungsten filament (melting point: 3380¬∞C) heats up and emits light when current passes through. Bulb is filled with inert gas (argon/nitrogen) to prevent filament oxidation. The glass bulb prevents oxygen from reaching the hot filament.

3. Electric Heater

Uses nichrome coil (high resistance) wound on ceramic material. Current through the coil produces heat, making it glow red. Used for space heating, water heating, cooking, etc.

4. Electric Fuse

‚ö° Electric Fuse

A safety device that protects circuits by melting when excessive current flows, thereby breaking the circuit and preventing damage to appliances or fire.

Fuse characteristics:

  • Connected in series with the device
  • Made of low melting point alloy (tin, lead, aluminum)
  • Rated as 1A, 2A, 3A, 5A, 10A etc. based on appliance power
  • Melts when current exceeds rated value (due to heating effect)
  • Once melted, needs replacement (unlike circuit breakers)

Safety Note: Never replace a fuse with a higher rating or with copper wire, as this defeats its safety purpose and can cause fire.
