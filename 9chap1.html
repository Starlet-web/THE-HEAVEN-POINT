Introduction to Cells

  • Discovery of Cell: Robert Hooke (1665) saw in thin slice of cork ,honey comb like structure using primitive microscope.He called them cell.
  • Leeuwenhoek [1674] discovered free living cell in pond water .[AnimalCule]
    • Cell Cell is the Structural,Functional and fundametal unit of Life.
  • Cell Theory: Proposed by Schleiden and Schwann (1838-39), later expanded by Virchow (1855):
    1. All living organisms are composed of cells
    2. Cell is the basic unit of life
    3. All cells arise from pre-existing cells[Omni-cellula-e-cellula]

    • Important Discoveries

    Scientist Discovery Year
    Robert Hooke First observed cells 1665
    Leeuwenhoek First observed living cells 1674
    Robert Brown Discovered nucleus 1831
    Purkinje Discovere Protoplasm 1839
    Timeline Tip: Remember the progression: First saw dead cells (Hooke, 1665) â†’ then living cells (Leeuwenhoek, 1674) â†’ discovered nucleus (Brown, 1831) â†’ established cell theory (Schleiden & Schwann, 1838-39).
  • Types of Cells:
    • Prokaryotic: No nucleus (e.g., bacteria)
    • Eukaryotic: True nucleus (e.g., plant and animal cells)

    • Cell Structure


    [Interactive Diagram: Typical Plant and Animal Cells]

    Cell Membrane

    • Semi-permeable phospholipid bilayer
    • Functions: Protection, selective transport, cell recognition

    Cell Wall (Plant Cells Only)

    • Rigid outer layer made of cellulose
    • Functions: Structural support, protection, prevents bursting

    Cytoplasm

    • Jelly-like substance between nucleus and cell membrane
    • Contains organelles and cytoskeleton

    Nucleus

    • Control center of the cell
    • Contains chromatin (DNA + proteins)
    • Surrounded by nuclear membrane with pores
    • Contains nucleolus (ribosome production)

    Cell Organelles

    Mitochondria

    • Mitochondria are small, cylindrical organelles present in cytoplasm.
    • It is double membranous structure.[Outer/inner]
    • The inner membrane is folded into finger-like structures called cristae.
    • The space inside cristae is called matrix.
    • They are Responsible for cellular respiration which provides energy.
    • So mitochondria is also called "Power House Of The Cell."
    • It was discovered by Richard Altmann.

    [Diagram: Mitochondria Structure - The Powerhouse]

    Plastids

    • It is double membranous structure in plant cell.
    • It is Responsible for manufacturing and storage of food.
    • It was discovered by Ernst Haeckel.
    • It is of three types:
      1. Chloroplast
      2. Chromoplast
      3. Leucoplast
    • Chromoplast
      - Responsible for non-green pigments (red, yellow, orange).
      - Found in flowers and fruits (e.g., tomatoes, carrots).
      - Attracts pollinators and seed dispersers.
    • Leucoplast (corrected from "Legionless")
      - Colorless plastids for storage.
      - Types include:
        â€¢ Amyloplasts (store starch)
        â€¢ Elaioplasts (store lipids)
        â€¢ Proteinoplasts (store proteins)
      - Found in roots and storage tissues.
    • Chloroplast
      - It is present only in plant cell and algae. It contains chlorophyll that is responsible for green colours in leaves. Chlorophyll absorbs sunlight thus helps in photosynthesis. Inside the inner membrane fluid substance is a discret. In stroma are like structures is thalkoid which bears chlorophyll. Groups of thalkoid is gramum. It is also called Kitchen of the cell.

    [Diagram: Plastid Types - Nature's Color Palette]

    Golgi Apparatus

    • It was first discovered by scientist Camillo Golgi.
    • It is a membrane bound organelle made up of series of flattened stacked pouches called cisternae.
    • It is located in the cytoplasm next to the endoplasmic reticulum.
    • Golgi body is also called the "Traffic Police Of Cell".
    • Functions:
      1. It helps in storage, modification and release of cell products.
      2. Complex sugars are made from simple sugars in Golgi body.
      3. It helps in formation of lysosome.

    [Diagram: Golgi Apparatus - The Cell's Post Office]

    Endoplasmic Reticulum

    • It was described by Keith R. Porter.
    • It is a network of tubes and sheets made up of cisternae, tubules and vesicles.
    • There are two types of ER:
      1. Smooth Endoplasmic Reticulum
      2. Rough Endoplasmic Reticulum
    • Smooth Endoplasmic Reticulum:
      • It is smooth because ribosomes aren't attached on its surface.
      • They are sites of fat molecule synthesis.
    • Rough Endoplasmic Reticulum:
      • It is rough because ribosomes are attached on its surface.
      • They are sites of protein synthesis.
    • Functions:
      1. It serves as channel for transport of molecules between cytoplasm and nucleus.
      2. SER helps in fat molecule synthesis and RER helps in protein synthesis.
      3. It helps in detoxification.

    [Diagram: Endoplasmic Reticulum - The Cell's Highway System]

    Lysosomes

    • It was discovered by Christian de Duve.
    • Lysosome is a spherical sac-like single membranous structure.
    • Lysosomes are filled with about 50 different types of digestive enzymes called acid hydrolases.
    • Lysosomes are highly polymorphic cell organelles because they are found in different forms.
    • Functions:
      1. It helps in digestion of foreign materials received in cell.
      2. It digests old and dead cell organelles.
      3. It is also known as "suicidal bag," because sometimes all lysosomes of a cell burst completely to dissolve the cell organelles.

    [Diagram: Lysosome - The Cell's Recycling Center]

    Ribosomes

    • It was discovered by George Palade.
    • Ribosomes are minute particles present in cytoplasm.
    • It is non-membranous organelle.
    • There are two types of ribosomes based on their sedimentation units:
      1. 70S type:
        • Found in prokaryotic cells
        • Has two subunits: 50S and 30S
      2. 80S type:
        • Found in eukaryotic cells
        • Has two subunits: 60S and 40S
    • Function: It synthesizes proteins so it is also called protein factory of the cell.

    [Diagram: Ribosomes - The Protein Factories]

    Vacuoles

    • Vacuoles are fluid-filled cavities present in the cytoplasm.
    • The fluid inside vacuoles is called cell sap.
    • They are surrounded by a single membrane called tonoplast.
    • In mature plant cells, there is one large central vacuole.
    • In animal cells, vacuoles if present are much smaller.
    • The vacuole fluid is viscous and contains various substances.
    • Functions:
      1. They help to maintain osmotic pressure in the cell.
      2. They act as storage house of pigments, waste products, useful minerals and salts.

    [Diagram: Vacuole - The Cell's Storage Tank]

    Differences Between Plant and Animal Cells

    Feature Plant Cell Animal Cell
    Cell Wall Present (cellulose) Absent
    Plastids Present Absent
    Vacuole Large central vacuole Small/absent
    Centrioles Absent Present
    Lysosomes Rare Present
    Shape Fixed (rectangular) Irregular
    Memory Tip:

    "Plants Wear Caps" - Remember that Plant cells have Cell wall, Chloroplasts, and large central vacuoles (the "caps" they wear that animal cells don't).

    Cell Transport Mechanisms

    Diffusion

    • Movement of particles from high to low concentration
    • Example: Oxygen entering cells

    Osmosis

    • Diffusion of water through semi-permeable membrane
    • Types of solutions:
      • Isotonic: Equal concentration
      • Hypotonic: Lower solute concentration
      • Hypertonic: Higher solute concentration
    • Example: Raisins swelling in water
    Osmosis Memory Tip:

    "Hippo" - Water moves into cell in Hypotonic solution (cell swells like a hippo). In Hypertonic solution, water moves out (cell shrinks).

    Real-world Connection: Osmosis explains why your fingers get wrinkly in water (water moves into skin cells) and why salted vegetables release water (salt creates hypertonic environment).

    Cell Division

    Mitosis

    • Occurs in somatic (body) cells
    • Produces two identical daughter cells
    • Maintains chromosome number (2n â†’ 2n)
    • Phases: Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase

    Meiosis

    • Occurs in germ cells (reproductive cells)
    • Produces four non-identical gametes
    • Reduces chromosome number (2n â†’ n)
    • Two divisions: Meiosis I and Meiosis II

    [Interactive Diagram: Comparing Mitosis and Meiosis]
    Mitosis Phase Mnemonic:

    "PMAT" - Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase (the order of mitosis phases)
