Light: Reflection and Refraction

Introduction to Light

Light is a form of energy that enables us to see objects. It is an electromagnetic radiation that can be detected by the human eye.

  • Light travels in straight lines (rectilinear propagation of light).
  • The speed of light in vacuum is 3 √ó 108 m/s.
  • Light exhibits both wave-like and particle-like properties (wave-particle duality).

Reflection of Light

When light falls on a surface, the direction of the ray is changed. This change in direction is called reflection.

Laws of Reflection:

  1. The angle of incidence (i) is equal to the angle of reflection (r).
  2. The incident ray, the reflected ray and the normal to the reflecting surface at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.
Activity: Take a comb and close all its openings except one. Hold it perpendicular to a sheet of paper. Direct a beam of light from a torch through the opening of the comb. Observe the path of light. Now place a plane mirror in the path of this light. Observe the change in direction of light after reflection.

Types of Images

An image is formed when light rays coming from an object meet or appear to meet after reflection or refraction.

Real Image Virtual Image
Formed when light rays actually meet Formed when light rays appear to meet (but don't actually meet)
Can be obtained on a screen Cannot be obtained on a screen
Always inverted Always erect
Formed by concave mirrors and convex lenses Formed by plane mirrors, convex mirrors and concave lenses

Spherical Mirrors

A spherical mirror is a mirror whose reflecting surface is part of a hollow sphere. There are two types:

  • Concave Mirror: Reflecting surface is the inner curved surface (converging mirror)
  • Convex Mirror: Reflecting surface is the outer bulged surface (diverging mirror)

Important Terms:

  • Pole (P): The center point of the mirror's reflecting surface.
  • Center of Curvature (C): The center of the sphere of which the mirror is a part.
  • Radius of Curvature (R): The distance between the pole and center of curvature.
  • Principal Axis: The straight line joining the pole and center of curvature.
  • Focus (F): The point where parallel rays of light meet or appear to meet after reflection.
  • Focal Length (f): The distance between the pole and focus.
Focal length (f) = R/2

Image Formation by Concave Mirrors

The nature of image formed by a concave mirror depends on the position of the object.

Position of Object Position of Image Size of Image Nature of Image
At infinity At the focus F Highly diminished Real and inverted
Beyond C Between F and C Diminished Real and inverted
At C At C Same size Real and inverted
Between C and F Beyond C Enlarged Real and inverted
At F At infinity Highly enlarged Real and inverted
Between P and F Behind the mirror Enlarged Virtual and erect

Uses of Concave Mirrors:

  • Shaving mirrors to get enlarged image
  • As reflectors in torches, headlights of vehicles
  • By dentists to examine teeth
  • In solar furnaces to concentrate sunlight

Image Formation by Convex Mirrors

Convex mirrors always form virtual, erect and diminished images regardless of the object's position.

Position of Object Position of Image Size of Image Nature of Image
At infinity At the focus F, behind the mirror Highly diminished Virtual and erect
Between infinity and pole Between P and F, behind the mirror Diminished Virtual and erect

Uses of Convex Mirrors:

  • As rear-view mirrors in vehicles because they give a wider field of view
  • At blind turns and dangerous road bends
  • In shops and supermarkets for security purposes

Mirror Formula and Magnification

The relationship between object distance (u), image distance (v) and focal length (f) is given by the mirror formula:

1/f = 1/v + 1/u

Where:
f = focal length of the mirror
v = distance of image from the mirror
u = distance of object from the mirror

Magnification

Magnification (m) is the ratio of the height of the image to the height of the object.

m = hi/ho = -v/u

Where:
hi = height of image
ho = height of object

Refraction of Light

Refraction is the bending of light when it passes from one medium to another with different optical densities.

Laws of Refraction:

  1. The incident ray, the refracted ray and the normal to the interface at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane.
  2. The ratio of sine of angle of incidence to the sine of angle of refraction is constant for a given pair of media (Snell's Law).
sin i / sin r = constant (Œº)

This constant is called the refractive index of the second medium with respect to the first medium.

Activity: Place a coin at the bottom of a bucket filled with water. With your eye at a position above the water, try to pick up the coin. You'll find that the coin appears to be at a higher position than it actually is. This is due to refraction of light.

Refractive Index

The refractive index of a medium is a measure of how much it can bend light.

Œº = speed of light in vacuum / speed of light in medium = c/v

Relative Refractive Index:

The refractive index of medium 2 with respect to medium 1 is given by:

1Œº2 = Œº2/Œº1 = v1/v2

Where:
Œº1 = absolute refractive index of medium 1

Refraction through a Rectangular Glass Slab:

  • When light passes through a rectangular glass slab, it undergoes refraction twice.
  • The emergent ray is parallel to the incident ray but laterally displaced.
  • The angle of incidence equals the angle of emergence (i = e).

Spherical Lenses

A lens is a transparent material (usually glass) bounded by two surfaces, at least one of which is spherical. There are two types:

  • Convex Lens: Thicker at the center than at the edges (converging lens)
  • Concave Lens: Thinner at the center than at the edges (diverging lens)

Important Terms:

  • Optical Center (O): The central point of the lens.
  • Principal Axis: The line joining the centers of curvature of the two surfaces.
  • Focus (F): The point where parallel rays of light meet or appear to meet after refraction.
  • Focal Length (f): The distance between the optical center and focus.

Uses of Convex Lenses:

  • In cameras to focus light and form images
  • In microscopes and telescopes to magnify objects
  • As magnifying glasses
  • To correct hypermetropia (long-sightedness)

Image Formation by Concave Lenses

A concave lens always forms virtual, erect and diminished images regardless of the object's position.

Uses of Concave Lenses:

  • In spectacles to correct myopia (short-sightedness)
  • In peepholes of doors
  • In some types of telescopes

Power of a Lens

The power of a lens is a measure of its ability to converge or diverge light rays.

P = 1/f

Where:
P = power of lens (in dioptre, D)
f = focal length (in meters)

  • The SI unit of power is dioptre (D).
  • Convex lens has positive power (+P).
  • Concave lens has negative power (-P).
  • For combination of lenses, the net power is the algebraic sum of individual powers.
Pnet = P1 + P2 + P3 + ...
