Timeline of Key Discoveries:

Prout's Hypothesis (1815)

William Prout proposed that all elements are made from hydrogen atoms (called "protyles").

Atomic weight = n √ó (hydrogen's atomic weight)

Why it failed: Discoveries of elements with non-integer atomic weights (e.g., Chlorine: 35.45) disproved this.

D√∂bereiner's Triads (1829)

Groups of three elements with similar properties where the middle element's atomic weight was the average of the others.

Example: Li (6.94), Na (22.99), K (39.10)

(6.94 + 39.10)/2 ‚âà 23.02 ‚âà Na's atomic weight (22.99)

Limitation: Only worked for certain elements (e.g., failed for F, Cl, Br triad).

Newlands' Law of Octaves (1864)

Noticed that every 8th element had similar properties (like musical octaves).

Li Be B C N O F

Na Mg Al Si P S Cl

Problem: Worked only up to calcium (Ca) and couldn't accommodate noble gases.

Mendeleev's Periodic Table (1869)

Arranged elements by atomic weight with periodic properties, leaving gaps for undiscovered elements.

Successful Predictions:

  • Eka-aluminum ‚Üí Gallium (Ga)
  • Eka-silicon ‚Üí Germanium (Ge)
  • Eka-boron ‚Üí Scandium (Sc)

Modern Periodic Law

Key Experiment: Henry Moseley's X-ray spectroscopy (1913) proved atomic number (Z) is fundamental.

\[ \sqrt{v} \propto Z \]

where \( v \) = X-ray frequency, \( Z \) = atomic number

The Modern Statement

"Properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers."

Atomic Properties & Trends

Atomic Radius

Distance from nucleus to outermost electrons.

Measurement Methods:

  • Covalent radius: ¬Ω bond length between identical atoms
  • van der Waals radius: ¬Ω distance between non-bonded atoms
  • Metallic radius: ¬Ω distance between metal atoms

Periodic Trends

Across a Period:

Atomic radius ‚Üí decreases

Reason: Increasing Zeff pulls electrons closer

Example: Na (186 pm) > Mg (160 pm) > Al (143 pm)

Down a Group:

Atomic radius ‚Üí increases

Reason: Additional electron shells

Example: Li (152 pm) < Na (186 pm) < K (227 pm)

Exceptions:
  • Ga (135 pm) < Al (143 pm) due to d-block contraction
  • Lanthanide contraction makes 5d elements similar in size to 4d

Electron Affinity

Energy released when an electron is added to gaseous atom.

\[ \text{Cl(g)} + e^- \rightarrow \text{Cl}^-\text{(g)} \quad (\Delta H = -\text{ve}) \]

Highest EA: Cl (349 kJ/mol) > F (328 kJ/mol)

Reason: F's small size causes electron repulsion

Noble Gases: EA ‚âà 0

Reason: Stable octet configuration

Electronegativity

Tendency of an atom to attract shared electrons in a bond.

Across Period:

Electronegativity ‚Üí increases

Reason: Increasing nuclear charge

Example: Li (0.98) < C (2.55) < F (3.98)

Down Group:

Electronegativity ‚Üí decreases

Reason: Increasing atomic size

Example: F (3.98) > Cl (3.16) > Br (2.96)

Practice Problems

Test your understanding with these challenges:

Problem 1: Atomic Size

Arrange in order of increasing atomic radius:

B, C, Al, Si

Answer: C < B < Si < Al

Explanation: Across period (C < B), down group (B < Al), and Si is smaller than Al due to higher Zeff.

Problem 2: Ionization Energy

Answer: Mg has a stable 3s¬≤ configuration, while Al loses one p-electron (3s¬≤ 3p¬π).

Detailed: The 3s¬≤ configuration in Mg is more stable than 3s¬≤3p¬π in Al, requiring more energy to remove an electron.
