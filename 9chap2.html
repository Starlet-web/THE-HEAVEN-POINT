Matter and Mixtures

Mohit Sir(Msc)

Pure Matter

Pure matter: It is the substance which has uniform chemical composition throughout the compound and it can't be separated or broken into simpler form by physical forces.

Examples: Elements like calcium, potassium, oxygen; compounds like water, salt.

Elements

Elements are made up of single type of atoms and can't be broken down into simpler substances.

Classification of Elements

Type Characteristics Examples
Metals Shiny, conductive, malleable Iron, cobalt, zinc, sodium
Non-metals Dull, poor conductors, brittle Chlorine (Cl), iodine (I), fluorine (F), sulfur (S)
Metalloids Properties between metals and non-metals Boron, silicon, germanium

Compounds

Compounds are made up of a group of atoms that are tightly bound together by chemical bonds and can't be broken down into simpler substances by physical means.

Example: Water (H₂O), sodium chloride (NaCl)

Impure Matter

Impure matter can be broken down into simpler substances by physical processes.

Example: Mixtures like air, sea water

Mixtures

When two or more substances mix with each other without participating in a chemical change, the resulting substance is called a mixture.

Properties of Mixtures

  • The components of the mixture keep their original properties
  • The separation of components can be easily done
  • The proportion of the components is variable

Examples of Mixtures

Mixture Components
Crude oil Mixture of organic compounds (mainly hydrocarbons)
Sea water Mixture of various salts and water
Air Mixture of gases like oxygen, carbon dioxide, nitrogen
Ink Mixture of colored dyes
Gunpowder Mixture of sulfur, potassium nitrate and carbon

Types of Solutions

Based on Amount of Solute

Type Definition
Saturated solution A solution containing the maximum amount of solute dissolved in a solvent at a given temperature
Unsaturated solution A solution containing less than the maximum amount of solute that can be dissolved

Note: The solubility of a solution is affected by:

  • The nature of the solute and solvent
  • Temperature
  • Pressure
  • Presence of other chemicals in solution

Based on Solvent

Type Definition Examples

Non-aqueous solution The solution in which solvent is not water (may be polar or non-polar) Iodine in alcohol

Colloidal Solutions

Colloidal solution is a heterogeneous mixture consisting of two phases:

  • Dispersed phase: Component present in small proportion
  • Dispersion medium: Component present in large proportion

Brownian Motion

The random zig-zag motion displayed by small particles suspended in fluids, especially in colloidal solutions. It is due to the collision of particles with molecules of the dispersion medium.

Tyndall Effect

When a beam of light passes through a colloidal solution, the path of the beam is illuminated in all directions. This illuminated path is called the "Tyndall cone".

The phenomenon of scattering of light by colloidal particles is called the "Tyndall effect".

Concentration of Solutions

Concentration is the amount of solute dissolved in a certain amount of solvent or solution.

Concentration of solution = amount of solute / amount of solvent

Mass Percentage (w/w)

Mass of solute in grams present in 100 grams of solution.

Formula:

Mass % = (Mass of solute / Mass of solution) × 100

where Mass of solution = Mass of solute + Mass of solvent

Example Calculation 1

A solution contains 40g of common salt in 320g of water. Calculate the mass percentage.

Mass of solute (salt) = 40g

Mass of solvent (water) = 320g

Mass of solution = 40g + 320g = 360g

Mass % = (40/360) × 100 = 11.11%

Example Calculation 2

3.5g of sugar is dissolved in 46.5g of water. Calculate the mass percentage.

Mass of solute (sugar) = 3.5g

Mass of solvent (water) = 46.5g

Mass of solution = 3.5g + 46.5g = 50g

Mass % = (3.5/50) × 100 = 7%

Volume Percentage (v/v)

Volume of solute in mL present in 100mL of solution.

Formula:

Volume % = (Volume of solute / Volume of solution) × 100

Chemical vs Physical Changes

Change Type Characteristics Examples
Chemical Change
  • Permanent change
  • New substances are formed
  • Difficult to reverse
  • Chemical composition changes
 Burning, rusting, digestion
Physical Change
  • Temporary change
  • No new substances are formed
  • Easy to reverse
  • Chemical composition remains same
  • Physical properties might change (color, state, shape, size, melting point, etc.)
 Melting, freezing, dissolving
